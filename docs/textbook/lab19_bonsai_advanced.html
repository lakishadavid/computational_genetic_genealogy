Lab 19: Advanced Construction Techniques in Bonsai

Why This Matters: The algorithms used to reconstruct family relationships from genetic data embody powerful assumptions about how kinship is structured and valued. Advanced construction techniques in Bonsai reveal how computational approaches to family history are never neutral technical processes but involve profound choices about which relationships matter, whose histories are recoverable, and what counts as valid evidence of connection. These techniques offer both promise and peril: the potential to reconnect deliberately severed lineages while simultaneously risking the imposition of culturally-specific family structures onto diverse communities.

Learning Objectives

  • Analyze how search strategies in pedigree reconstruction embody particular cultural assumptions about family structure
  • Examine the ethical implications of incorporating historical and demographic constraints into algorithmic kinship inference
  • Critically evaluate approaches to endogamy across different cultural contexts and their implications for community identity
  • Consider how ancestral population reconstruction interacts with contemporary politics of belonging
  • Explore the epistemological foundations of parameter optimization in relationship inference
  • Develop frameworks for algorithmic adaptation that center community needs and diverse kinship systems

Navigating Possibility Spaces

What computational genealogists call the "pedigree search space" is more accurately understood as a possibility space for kinship—a mathematical representation of all possible ways people might be connected. How algorithms navigate this space reveals profound assumptions about what kinds of families matter.

Cultural Assumptions in Search Space Construction

The very concept of a pedigree "search space" encodes particular culturally specific assumptions about how human relationships should be organized:

  • Nuclear Family Centrism: The search space typically assumes nuclear family units as the building blocks of larger kinship networks, privileging parent-child bonds over other forms of relatedness
  • Bilateral Descent: Standard pedigree models assume bilateral descent (tracing ancestry equally through maternal and paternal lines), which may not align with unilineal kinship systems that prioritize one line
  • Generational Hierarchy: The search space is typically organized into distinct generations with clear boundaries, potentially obscuring age-set systems or cultural practices with more fluid generational boundaries
  • Biological Determinism: The very premise of the search space assumes that genetic relatedness maps unproblematically onto social kinship, when many cultural systems distinguish between these forms of relationship

These assumptions are not merely theoretical—they are encoded into the very algorithms that determine which possible family structures are considered plausible and which are rendered invisible or impossible by the computational framework.

Political Dimensions of Search Strategies

Different search strategies for navigating the pedigree space embody different values and priorities about which connections matter most:

Strategy Technical Description Embedded Values Communities Potentially Privileged/Marginalized
Greedy Search Prioritizes locally optimal choices at each step Values certainty and immediate connections over complex, ambiguous relationships Privileges communities with well-documented recent history; may marginalize those with fragmented historical records
Beam Search Maintains multiple competing hypotheses simultaneously Acknowledges multiplicity of possible interpretations; tolerates uncertainty Better serves communities with complex, interwoven kinship histories, though still within cultural constraints
Iterative Deepening Gradually explores deeper generational connections Privileges relationships in proportion to their genealogical "closeness" May disadvantage communities seeking to recover distant connections disrupted by historical trauma
Simulated Annealing Makes probabilistic moves with decreasing randomness Accepts temporary "disorder" to find better global structures More amenable to non-Western kinship systems that don't fit standard genealogical models
Monte Carlo Methods Samples space based on probabilistic models Embraces uncertainty as fundamental to relationship inference May better represent communities with oral rather than written traditions, where relationships involve inherent ambiguity

The choice of search strategy is never merely technical efficiency but represents a stance on what kinds of evidence are valued, how uncertainty should be handled, and which relationships deserve priority in reconstruction efforts. For communities seeking to recover connections disrupted by slavery, genocide, or forced removal, these algorithmic choices can determine whether their histories become recoverable or remain obscured.

Heuristics as Cultural Knowledge

Heuristics—the rules of thumb used to guide search—encode specific cultural knowledge about what constitutes plausible family structures:

Heuristics like prioritizing connections based on total IBD sharing or using age information to constrain parent-child relationships may appear as neutral optimization techniques. However, these encode specific assumptions about how families typically form: minimum parental ages (e.g., 15 years), maximum age differences between parents and children (e.g., 50 years), and expectations about which relationships tend to share more genetic material.

These numerical thresholds emerge from particular historical contexts and family formation patterns that may not be universal across cultures or historical periods. For example, the common heuristic of a 15-50 year parental age range encodes modern Western expectations about childbearing, potentially misrepresenting historical contexts where marriage and childbearing occurred at different ages or contemporary communities with different family formation patterns.

Similarly, heuristics that prioritize strong genetic links (e.g., setting minimum IBD thresholds for "strong links") encode assumptions about which relationships warrant attention—typically privileging close biological connections over more distant ones or connections maintained through other forms of kinship like adoption, fictive kinship, or community belonging.

Constraining Genealogical Possibility

Constraints in Bonsai limit the universe of possible kinship configurations based on different forms of evidence, raising questions about whose knowledge counts in determining family structure.

Documented Relationships as Colonial Archives

Using known relationships as constraints in pedigree reconstruction requires critical reflection on the politics of documentation:

The ability to specify "known relationships" with high confidence values (e.g., 0.95 for well-documented connections) privileges certain forms of evidence—typically written records, legal documentation, and officially recognized kinship structures—over others like oral histories, community recognition, or alternative kinship systems. This approach tends to reify colonial archival practices that documented certain kinds of relationships while ignoring or actively erasing others.

For many communities whose histories were deliberately suppressed, official documentation may be sparse, inaccurate, or actively harmful. Indigenous peoples whose traditional kinship systems were targeted for dismantling, enslaved peoples whose family connections were deliberately broken, and communities subjected to forced assimilation all face historical records that may contradict rather than confirm actual family connections.

When algorithmic approaches like Bonsai treat documented relationships as ground truth constraints, they risk reproducing these historical injustices rather than helping to recover suppressed histories. A more nuanced approach would recognize the political nature of documentation itself and develop ways to incorporate alternative forms of knowledge about kinship alongside written records.

Temporal Constraints and Historical Contingency

Temporal constraints based on birth and death dates encode assumptions about life courses that vary across historical contexts:

Adding temporal constraints to Bonsai frames kinship within particular understandings of human life courses and reproduction, often derived from contemporary Western expectations. Parameters like "minimum reproductive age" and "maximum reproductive age" appear as neutral biological constants but actually represent historically and culturally specific norms about family formation that have varied considerably across time and place.

The temporal organization of constraining birth and death years into neat parameters creates a model of human life that may not capture the complexity and contingency of actual historical experience. Families separated by war, displacement, or enslavement may have reproduction patterns that defy standard expectations. Communities practicing adoption, child fostering, or other forms of shared child-rearing may have parent-child relationships that don't align with biological reproductive constraints.

When using such constraints, researchers must consider how demographic assumptions might impose contemporary or culturally-specific expectations onto historical contexts where different patterns of family formation prevailed. This is particularly important when working with communities whose historical experiences include disrupted reproduction patterns due to genocide, slavery, war, or forced removal.

Geographical Constraints and Mobility Politics

Geographical constraints in pedigree reconstruction raise important questions about human mobility and its restriction:

Adding geographical constraints to relationship inference assumes particular relationships between physical proximity and family formation. Parameters like "max distance for relationship" encode assumptions about human mobility that have been radically transformed by historical forces from colonization to globalization, forced displacement to voluntary migration.

These constraints can be particularly problematic when applied to communities whose geographical distribution has been shaped by violent historical processes. For African diaspora communities, geographical constraints might make it difficult to recognize transatlantic family connections disrupted by the slave trade. For Indigenous peoples subjected to forced relocation, such constraints might fail to capture pre-removal kinship networks or connections maintained despite geographical separation.

At the same time, thoughtful application of geographical constraints might help recover connections obscured by historical processes. Understanding historical patterns of restricted mobility—who could move where and when—might help identify likely family connections within those constraints, while also highlighting how kinship networks adapted to and resisted these restrictions.

A critical approach to geographical constraints would recognize both the historical realities of restricted mobility and the agency of communities in maintaining connections across imposed boundaries and displacements.

Endogamy and Community Boundaries

Approaches to handling endogamy in computational genealogy reveal deeper questions about how community boundaries are defined, maintained, and transformed through patterns of marriage and reproduction.

Endogamy Factors as Community Identity

The statistical treatment of endogamy in Bonsai reflects and reinforces particular understandings of community formation and boundary maintenance:

When Bonsai assigns "endogamy factors" to different populations (e.g., 5.0 for Ashkenazi Jewish, 3.0 for Finnish), it quantifies and formalizes cultural practices of marriage within communities. These numerical factors appear as neutral statistical corrections but actually encode judgments about the "exceptionality" of different communities relative to an implicit norm. The positioning of European populations as the reference point (endogamy factor 1.0) reveals how these adjustments frame certain communities as "standard" and others as deviations requiring correction.

These adjustments also tend to reify population categories, treating communities like "Ashkenazi Jewish" or "Finnish" as stable, coherent groups with clear boundaries rather than dynamic, heterogeneous communities with complex histories of interaction with neighboring groups. The very practice of assigning discrete endogamy factors to named populations risks reproducing problematic understandings of human groups as genetically distinct and internally homogeneous.

At the same time, accounting for different historical patterns of endogamy is necessary for accurate relationship inference in many communities. The challenge lies in how to represent these patterns without reinforcing essentialist notions of population difference or treating certain communities as exceptions to an unstated norm.

Pedigree Collapse and Historical Context

Handling pedigree collapse requires understanding the diverse historical contexts in which communities practiced endogamy:

The phenomenon of "pedigree collapse"—where the same ancestor appears multiple times in a family tree—is often treated as a technical challenge requiring specialized algorithms. But pedigree collapse emerges from particular historical conditions and community practices that deserve contextual understanding rather than mere technical accommodation.

Communities practiced endogamy for diverse reasons: religious solidarity, cultural preservation, economic necessity, geographical isolation, or in response to external exclusion. Ashkenazi Jewish endogamy developed partly in response to European anti-Semitism and forced segregation. Island or isolated communities developed endogamous practices due to geographical constraints. Many Indigenous communities practiced specific forms of cousin marriage to strengthen clan alliances.

When algorithms detect and handle pedigree collapse, they are engaging with these complex histories. Parameters like "collapse_likelihood_threshold" encode judgments about when to recognize someone as the same ancestor appearing through multiple pathways, directly engaging questions of identity and relationship that have deep cultural significance.

A more critically informed approach would recognize pedigree collapse not merely as a statistical phenomenon to be corrected but as evidence of particular community histories that should be understood in their specific contexts.

Multiple Ancestral Paths as Cultural Practice

The technical challenge of individuals occupying multiple roles in a pedigree reflects deeper questions about kinship flexibility across cultures:

When Bonsai enables detection of "multiple ancestral paths" and "individuals with multiple roles," it creates space for recognizing kinship systems more complex than simple bilateral descent patterns. This technical feature has profound implications for representing diverse cultural practices of kinship and marriage.

Many cultural traditions include marriage practices that create multiple relationship paths between individuals: cross-cousin marriage in many Indigenous communities, levirate marriage (where a man marries his deceased brother's widow) in various traditions, sororate marriage (where a man marries his deceased wife's sister) in others. These practices create kinship networks where individuals occupy multiple relationship roles simultaneously—being both a cousin and a spouse, or both an aunt and a stepmother.

The ability of Bonsai to represent these complex relationships through multiple ancestral paths potentially allows more accurate representation of diverse kinship systems. However, this representation remains constrained by the underlying model of pedigrees as collections of parent-child relationships—a model that may not capture the full complexity of kinship systems organized around different principles like clan membership, age-sets, or moiety systems.

Ancestry and Identity

Techniques for reconstructing ancestral populations engage directly with questions of identity, belonging, and the relationship between past and present communities.

Generation as Cultural Category

The computational representation of generations in multi-generational reconstruction involves particular understandings of how time and descent should be organized:

When Bonsai implements "generation-aware search" with parameters for "max_generations" and generation-specific confidence thresholds, it imposes a particular understanding of how human relationships should be organized across time. This generations are understood as distinct, countable units with clear boundaries—a conception that may conflict with more fluid understandings of generational connection in many cultural contexts.

The pattern of requiring "stricter evidence requirements for older generations" encodes an epistemological position about how certainty diminishes with temporal distance. While this aligns with certain statistical realities of genetic inheritance, it also represents a particular stance on how knowledge of the past should be evaluated—privileging recent connections over distant ones in ways that may not align with how all communities value their ancestral connections.

For communities whose cultural practices emphasize deep ancestral connections—where relationships to distant ancestors may hold equal or greater significance than recent ones—this algorithmic privileging of recent generations may misrepresent their understanding of kinship. Indigenous communities who trace clan relationships back many generations, or African diaspora communities seeking to recover connections across the Middle Passage, may value distant connections precisely because they have been systematically obscured by colonial powers.

Subpopulation Inference as Community Formation

The algorithmic identification of subpopulations engages directly with questions of how communities come to be defined and recognized:

When Bonsai "clusters individuals based on IBD patterns" to identify subpopulations, it engages in a form of algorithmic community formation—defining groups based on patterns of genetic sharing. This process raises profound questions about the relationship between genetic patterns and social identity, particularly in the context of communities who may define belonging through other means.

The language of "clusters," "members," and "links between subpopulations" creates a technological framework for understanding human groups that may align or conflict with how communities understand themselves. For some groups, this genetic clustering may confirm long-held understandings of community boundaries; for others, it may challenge or complicate narratives of belonging and identity.

The algorithmic identification of "links between subpopulations" engages with histories of inter-community contact, migration, and boundary maintenance that have deep social and political significance. Determining which individuals represent "links" between communities can have implications for contemporary questions of cultural brokerage, mixed heritage, and belonging in multiple communities.

A critically informed approach to subpopulation inference would recognize these algorithms not as neutral discoverers of pre-existing groups but as active participants in the ongoing social construction of community boundaries, with potential impacts on contemporary identity politics.

Demographic Inference as Historical Narrative

Inferring historical demographic parameters from reconstructed pedigrees creates particular forms of historical narrative that may empower or constrain community self-understanding:

When Bonsai calculates "population size over time," "average reproduction age," "inbreeding coefficients," and detects "bottlenecks" or "expansions," it translates complex human histories into a specific narrative frame focused on demographic parameters. This frame privileges certain aspects of history—population dynamics, reproduction patterns, migration rates—over others like cultural practices, resistance to oppression, or spiritual beliefs.

These demographic narratives can be both empowering and constraining for communities seeking to understand their histories. For groups whose historical narratives have been deliberately suppressed, demographic reconstruction might provide valuable evidence of population continuity despite genocidal violence, forced relocation, or enslavement. At the same time, reducing rich community histories to demographic parameters risks flattening complex experiences into abstract trends.

The detection of population "bottlenecks," for instance, engages directly with histories of genocide, epidemic disease, and colonial violence that have profound ongoing significance for affected communities. How these events are represented—as technical anomalies in population size or as traumatic historical injustices—matters deeply for how communities relate to their past.

A more critically engaged approach to demographic inference would recognize these algorithms as potential tools for community historical reclamation while remaining attentive to how their framing may reproduce or challenge colonial approaches to history.

Optimization and Epistemology

The optimization of algorithm parameters reveals deeper questions about what constitutes valid knowledge of human relationships and how certainty should be evaluated across different contexts.

Parameter Optimization as Value Judgment

The process of optimizing parameters for relationship inference involves implicit value judgments about what kinds of errors are acceptable:

When Bonsai performs "parameter sensitivity analysis" testing different values for "min_segment_length," "min_confidence," and other thresholds, it engages in a process of determining which types of uncertainty are tolerable in relationship inference. Each parameter choice involves trade-offs—between sensitivity and specificity, between finding more distant connections and avoiding false positives.

These trade-offs are not merely technical but deeply ethical, particularly in contexts where relationship inference has significant personal or political implications. For individuals seeking to reconnect with family separated by adoption, the cost of a false negative (failing to identify a true parent) may far outweigh the cost of a false positive. For Indigenous communities establishing tribal membership, different considerations might apply.

Even the choice of evaluation metrics reveals particular values. Using "likelihood" as the optimization target privileges statistical fit over other potential measures of success like alignment with community knowledge, emotional significance of connections, or balancing of different types of error. Using "accuracy" compared to a "true pedigree" assumes the existence of a ground truth that may not be available or may itself be contested.

A more transparent approach would make these value judgments explicit, allowing communities to participate in determining which parameters best align with their specific needs and priorities rather than assuming universal optimization criteria.

Adaptive Algorithms and Responsive Knowledge

Adaptive parameter tuning represents a particular approach to knowledge production that responds to emerging patterns in data:

When Bonsai implements "adaptive parameter tuning" that adjusts thresholds based on observed patterns, it embodies a specific epistemological stance—that knowledge of relationships should evolve responsively to the patterns discovered during analysis. This adaptive approach potentially creates space for algorithms to respond to the specific characteristics of different family structures and community histories rather than imposing universal standards.

The rules governing adaptation encode particular judgments about what constitutes a problem requiring adjustment. Rules like increasing confidence thresholds when "too many relationships" are found or raising endogamy factors when "elevated background IBD" is detected reflect specific understandings of what patterns are anomalous versus expected. The very definition of conditions like "too many relationships" or "high relationship uncertainty" encodes assumptions about normal family structures that may vary across cultural contexts.

Adaptive algorithms also raise questions about transparency and control. As parameters automatically adjust during analysis, the logic behind specific relationship inferences may become less transparent to users, potentially obscuring the reasoning behind particular conclusions about family connections.

A more critically informed approach would consider how adaptive processes might be designed to respond not just to statistical patterns but to incorporate community knowledge and priorities in determining which adaptations are appropriate in different contexts.

Research Goals as Political Projects

Different optimization strategies for different research goals reveal how technical choices align with broader political and social projects:

When Bonsai offers different optimization strategies for goals like "accurate_close_relatives," "distant_ancestry," "population_structure," and "demographic_inference," it acknowledges that relationship inference serves diverse purposes that require different technical approaches. These different configurations reveal how technical choices align with particular social and political projects around kinship and belonging.

The goal of "accurate_close_relatives" with its high confidence thresholds and focus on immediate family connections aligns with projects of family reunification, inheritance determination, or legal establishment of parentage. The goal of "distant_ancestry" with lower thresholds and deeper search connects to projects of diaspora reconnection, genealogical tourism, or cultural heritage recovery. "Population_structure" analysis serves projects of community boundary definition, historical migration reconstruction, or genetic ancestry testing. Each configuration engages different social questions with distinct ethical implications.

The presentation of these as neutral "research goals" with technical optimizations obscures their connection to particular political projects and value systems. The ability to easily optimize for certain goals over others creates differential access to different forms of kinship knowledge—making some kinds of connections more easily discoverable than others based on research priorities.

A more transparent approach would acknowledge the political dimensions of different research goals and consider whose interests are served by particular optimization strategies, creating space for communities to articulate their own priorities for kinship reconstruction.

Algorithmic Adaptation

The extension of algorithms through custom relationship models, likelihood functions, and external integrations creates possibilities for more culturally responsive approaches to computational genealogy.

Custom Relationship Models as Cultural Translation

The development of custom relationship models represents an opportunity for translating diverse kinship systems into computational frameworks:

When Bonsai allows registration of "custom relationship models" for relationships like "double first cousins" or complex combined relationships, it creates potential space for representing kinship systems that don't align with standard Western genealogical categories. This customization capability could allow for more accurate representation of the diverse ways human communities organize and understand their relationships.

Yet the underlying framework still requires translation of cultural kinship categories into the language of expected IBD sharing, segment counts, and variance parameters. This translation process itself involves epistemological assumptions about how cultural knowledge of kinship relates to genetic patterns. The requirement to specify technical parameters like "expected_total_ibd" and "variance_total_ibd" means that even custom relationships must be expressed in terms legible to the algorithm's statistical framework.

More profound challenges arise with kinship systems organized around principles fundamentally different from genetic descent—clan systems where belonging may be determined by cultural practices rather than biological descent, or systems of fictive kinship where relationships are established through ritual rather than reproduction. The extent to which these can be meaningfully represented within Bonsai's framework, even with custom models, remains an open question.

A more collaborative approach would involve working directly with communities to develop relationship models that meaningfully translate their understanding of kinship into computational terms, while acknowledging the inherent limitations of this translation process.

Algorithmic Integration and Knowledge Sovereignty

Integration of genetic algorithms with external systems raises important questions about data sovereignty and knowledge control:

When Bonsai configures "external integrations" with genetic databases, mapping services, or demographic models, it engages questions of who controls, owns, and benefits from different forms of knowledge about human relationships. These integrations create data flows between different knowledge systems, each with its own politics of access, control, and representation.

Integration with external genetic databases raises questions about consent, privacy, and commercialization of genetic information. Access controlled through "auth_tokens" and "apikeys" creates particular governance structures for kinship knowledge that may align or conflict with community practices of knowledge sharing and protection. Integration with mapping services engages histories of cartographic knowledge that have often served colonial interests, raising questions about whose spatial understandings are represented in these systems.

For many communities, particularly Indigenous peoples, questions of data sovereignty—who controls information about community relationships and histories—are paramount. External integrations that place genetic information in dialogue with other knowledge systems create both opportunities for enriched understanding and risks of appropriation or misrepresentation.

A more ethical approach would engage explicitly with questions of data sovereignty and knowledge governance, ensuring that integrations between knowledge systems respect community protocols and ownership of their genetic and cultural information.

Exercises

  1. Analyze how a specific search strategy in Bonsai might advantage or disadvantage a particular community seeking to reconstruct relationships disrupted by historical trauma. Consider alternative approaches that might better serve this community's needs.
  2. Research a kinship system that differs significantly from Western bilateral descent patterns. How might Bonsai's representation of relationships need to be modified to accurately represent this system? What limitations might remain even with customization?
  3. Examine how the concept of "generations" is understood differently across cultural contexts. How might Bonsai's multigenerational reconstruction approach need to be adapted to respect these different understandings?
  4. Develop a framework for community consultation in parameter optimization that moves beyond technical criteria to incorporate community priorities and values. What process would enable communities to meaningfully participate in determining how relationship inference algorithms should work for their specific context?
  5. Consider a specific historical context where endogamy was practiced. What social, economic, religious, or political factors influenced this practice? How might understanding this context improve Bonsai's approach to handling elevated IBD sharing in this community?

Reflection: Advanced construction techniques in Bonsai reveal the profound interplay between technical methods and cultural understandings of kinship. While these algorithms offer powerful tools for reconstructing relationships obscured by historical trauma or migration, they also risk imposing particular frameworks of family and belonging that may not align with diverse cultural understandings. The most ethical and effective use of these techniques requires ongoing dialogue between technical development and community knowledge, creating space for computational approaches that respect the diversity of human kinship systems while helping communities reconnect relationships disrupted by historical injustice.
