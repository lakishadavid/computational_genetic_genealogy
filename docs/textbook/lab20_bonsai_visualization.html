Lab 20: The Politics of Visualization: Representing Kinship Systems

Why This Matters: Visualizations are never neutral representations but rather political acts that encode particular understandings of family, relationship, and belonging. How we choose to visualize pedigrees shapes how we understand kinship systems, reinforces or challenges normative assumptions about family structure, and makes particular aspects of relationship visible while obscuring others.

Learning Objectives

  • Critically analyze the cultural assumptions embedded in standard pedigree visualization conventions
  • Examine how different visualization approaches privilege particular understandings of kinship
  • Explore the ethical dimensions of representing uncertainty in genetic relationships
  • Consider how visualizations may reproduce or challenge inequitable power structures
  • Develop strategies for creating culturally responsive visualizations that honor diverse understandings of kinship
  • Reflect on how visualization choices shape narrative possibilities in genealogical research

The Cultural Politics of Pedigree Visualization

Standard pedigree visualizations encode particular cultural understandings of kinship that are not universal but rather reflect specific genealogical traditions and assumptions.

Encoding Gender in Visualization

The conventional visual language of pedigree charts—squares for males, circles for females, and diamonds for unknown sex—reflects binary understandings of gender that may not align with the lived experiences of all individuals or cultures. This binary encoding:

  • Erases non-binary, genderfluid, and transgender identities
  • Reinforces the centrality of biological sex in kinship systems
  • Privileges Western gender frameworks over indigenous gender systems (such as two-spirit identities)
  • Assumes gender is a primary organizing principle for kinship rather than one of many possible axes

These representational choices are not merely technical but deeply political, as they determine whose identities are legible within the visualization system and whose are rendered impossible to represent without distortion.

Hierarchical Layout and Power Relations

The dominant hierarchical layout of pedigree visualizations powerfully shapes how we understand kinship relations:

  • Vertical Hierarchies: Placing older generations at the top visually encodes authority flowing downward, reinforcing age-based power structures
  • Horizontal Ordering: The arrangement of siblings side-by-side often implicitly encodes birth order, creating visual hierarchies among siblings
  • Generation Spacing: Equal spacing between generations visually implies uniform temporal relationships that may erase complex histories of child-bearing at different ages or generation compression
  • Nuclear Family Centrality: The organization around parents and children privileges nuclear family structures over extended kinship networks or alternative family formations

These layouts are not neutral representations but rather encode particular cultural understandings of family structure that may not align with the kinship systems of the communities being represented.

Alternative Frameworks for Kinship Visualization

Different visualization approaches enable alternative understandings of kinship relations that may better reflect diverse cultural contexts and lived experiences.

Network-Centered Approaches

Network-centered visualizations shift focus from hierarchical parent-child relationships to the multi-dimensional web of connections between individuals:

  • Emphasize the interconnected nature of kinship beyond direct lineage
  • Allow for representation of chosen family and non-biological kinship
  • Highlight community structures rather than individual lineages
  • Provide space for visualizing complex intermarriage patterns and alliance systems
  • Challenge the primacy of vertical descent in favor of lateral connections

Network representations better capture the social reality of kinship as a dynamic system of relationships rather than a static hierarchy, allowing for the visualization of kinship as practiced rather than merely inherited.

Temporality and Historical Context

Integrating historical context into pedigree visualizations shifts attention from abstract genetic relationships to the lived experiences of individuals in particular sociohistorical contexts:

  • Places individual lives in historical context rather than abstracted generational time
  • Makes visible the impacts of historical events (wars, migrations, economic shifts) on family formation
  • Reveals patterns of differential longevity related to social inequities
  • Challenges ahistorical understandings of kinship that erase political conditions
  • Creates space for representing ruptures and discontinuities in family histories (such as those caused by enslavement or forced displacement)

Temporal visualizations challenge the abstraction of genetic kinship by situating relationships within the material and historical conditions that shaped them, reconnecting genetic genealogy to social history.

The Ethics of Uncertainty Visualization

How we choose to represent uncertainty in genetic relationships has profound ethical implications for the individuals and communities whose histories are being visualized.

Politics of Confidence Measures

Standard approaches to visualizing relationship confidence raise critical questions about how certainty is determined and represented:

  • Whose standards of evidence determine "high" versus "low" confidence relationships?
  • How do visualization systems handle relationships that are genealogically documented but genetically ambiguous?
  • What happens when documentary evidence contradicts genetic evidence, and how is this contradiction represented?
  • How might confidence visualizations reinforce scientific authority at the expense of community knowledge?
  • In what ways might color-coding confidence (e.g., red for low, green for high) encode moral judgments about family relationships?

These questions reveal how uncertainty visualization is never merely technical but rather deeply intertwined with questions of epistemic authority, evidence hierarchies, and whose knowledge counts in genealogical research.

Representing Non-normative Kinship

Conventional visualization systems often struggle to represent kinship formations that fall outside normative expectations:

  • Adoptive relationships may be marked as "different" or "less than" genetic relationships
  • Assisted reproductive technologies create kinship patterns that challenge visualization conventions
  • Step-relationships and blended families complicate the clean lines of traditional pedigrees
  • Cultural practices of informal adoption and child circulation are often difficult to represent
  • Diverse marriage patterns like polyandry or group marriage may be unrepresentable in standard systems

The limits of visualization systems reveal the normative assumptions about family structure that underlie computational approaches to genetic genealogy. When visualization systems cannot represent certain family forms, they effectively erase those kinship patterns from the genealogical record.

Geographical Imagination and Kinship Territories

The integration of geographical information into pedigree visualization raises complex questions about territory, belonging, and displacement in genealogical research.

Colonial Cartographies and Indigenous Territories

Map-based visualizations of pedigrees often uncritically reproduce colonial cartographies:

  • Nation-state boundaries are presented as natural rather than as products of colonial violence
  • Indigenous territorial understandings are erased in favor of settler state jurisdictions
  • Historical territorial changes may be flattened into contemporary political boundaries
  • The geographical positionality of the researcher shapes which territories are centered or marginalized
  • Mapping conventions inherited from colonial cartography may reproduce imperial ways of seeing

These visualization choices are deeply political, as they naturalize particular territorial arrangements while rendering others impossible to represent, with profound implications for how genealogical research engages with histories of displacement and dispossession.

Narratives of Migration and Belonging

Geographic visualizations of pedigrees create powerful narratives about migration and belonging:

  • Linear migration narratives may obscure histories of circular migration or forced displacement
  • The representation of "homeland" versus "diaspora" encodes assumptions about authentic belonging
  • Migration visualizations may reproduce nationalist narratives about assimilation and integration
  • Geographic ancestry representations can reify socially constructed racial categories as territorial realities
  • Map-based visualizations may privilege sedentary populations over nomadic communities

These visualization choices shape which migration narratives become visible and which are obscured, influencing how individuals understand their relationship to place, community, and identity through genetic genealogy.

Toward Justice-Oriented Visualization Practices

Developing ethical approaches to pedigree visualization requires rethinking conventional practices in ways that center justice, community needs, and diverse understandings of kinship.

Community-Centered Design

Just visualization practices require centering the needs, values, and kinship understandings of the communities whose histories are being represented:

  • Consulting with community members about how they understand and visualize their own kinship systems
  • Adapting visualization approaches to reflect culturally specific kinship terminologies and categories
  • Creating flexible systems that accommodate multiple ways of understanding and representing relationships
  • Ensuring communities have authority over how their kinship structures are represented
  • Developing collaborative visualization practices rather than imposing external standards

Community-centered visualization shifts authority from computational systems to the communities whose histories are being represented, recognizing that kinship is always culturally specific rather than universal.

Decolonizing Pedigree Representation

Decolonizing visualization practices requires challenging the Western genealogical traditions embedded in computational tools:

  • Centering indigenous kinship systems that may organize relationships differently than Western genealogy
  • Creating space for matrilineal, bilateral, and non-linear descent systems
  • Representing relationships beyond biological connection, including ceremonial kinship
  • Developing visualization approaches that can represent collective rather than individual identity
  • Acknowledging the colonial histories that shape what genealogical data is available and accessible

Decolonial visualization practices recognize that computational tools for genetic genealogy have emerged from specific cultural traditions and actively work to create space for alternative kinship systems that may better reflect diverse ways of understanding family and belonging.

Exercises

  1. Analyze a standard pedigree visualization and identify at least three cultural assumptions embedded in its representational choices.
  2. Design an alternative visualization approach for a family structure that challenges conventional pedigree representation (e.g., a blended family, a non-binary individual, or a community with collective child-rearing practices).
  3. Develop a visualization strategy that integrates both documentary and genetic evidence without privileging one over the other.
  4. Create a temporal visualization that situates a family history within the context of significant historical events and structural conditions.
  5. Collaborate with a community member to develop a visualization approach that better reflects their cultural understanding of kinship and belonging.

Reflection: Consider how the visualization tools and approaches we use shape the stories we can tell about kinship. What aspects of family history become visible or invisible based on our visualization choices? How might we develop more flexible and culturally responsive approaches to representing the complex realities of genetic and social kinship?
