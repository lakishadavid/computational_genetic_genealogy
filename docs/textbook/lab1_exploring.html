Lab 1: Exploring Population Genomic Data

Conceptual Foundations: This lab introduces the fundamental principles of population genomics that form the basis for genetic genealogy. Understanding genetic variation across populations provides the context necessary for interpreting individual genealogical connections.

Core Concepts

Human Genetic Variation

The human genome contains approximately 3 billion base pairs, but individuals differ at only about 0.1% of these positions. These differences, known as genetic variants, are the basis for both population genetics and genetic genealogy. Key points:

  • SNPs (Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms): Most common type of genetic variation where a single nucleotide differs between individuals
  • Alleles: Alternative forms of a genetic variant
  • Genotype: The specific combination of alleles an individual carries (e.g., AA, AG, or GG at a particular position)
  • Haplotype: A set of genetic variants that tend to be inherited together

When you analyze genetic data in the lab exercises, you're examining patterns in these variants across individuals and populations.

Population Genetic Structure

Human populations show genetic structure due to historical migration patterns, geographic isolation, and cultural factors. Understanding this structure is crucial for genetic genealogy because:

  • It provides context for interpreting an individual's genetic ancestry
  • It helps distinguish between genetic similarities due to recent common ancestry versus shared population history
  • It informs the expected patterns of genetic sharing between different populations

The 1000 Genomes Project data you'll explore in this lab provides a reference framework for global human genetic diversity, with samples categorized into five continental groups (Africa, Europe, East Asia, South Asia, and the Americas).

Genetic Data Formats

Understanding how genetic data is structured is essential for computational analysis:

  • VCF (Variant Call Format): The standard file format for representing genetic variants. Each row represents a genetic variant (e.g., a SNP), and each column represents an individual's genotype at that position.
  • Sample Metadata: Information about each sample, including population, family relationships, and quality metrics.
  • Genotype Representation: Typically coded as 0 (reference allele) and 1 (alternate allele), with diploid organisms having two values (e.g., 0|0, 0|1, or 1|1).

In the computational exercises, you'll learn to parse these formats and extract meaningful information using specialized libraries like allel.

Analytical Frameworks

Allele Frequency Analysis

Allele frequencies—the proportion of a specific allele in a population—are fundamental to understanding genetic variation:

  • They vary across populations due to evolutionary processes (natural selection, genetic drift, migration)
  • Population-specific allele frequencies serve as reference points for ancestry estimation
  • Rare variants (low frequency) are often more recent and may be informative for closer genealogical relationships

When calculating allele frequencies in the lab, you're quantifying how common different genetic variants are in each population, which provides insights into population history and structure.

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) in Population Genetics

PCA is a powerful dimension reduction technique that transforms complex genetic data into a smaller number of components that capture most of the variation:

  • In population genetics, PCA can visually represent genetic relationships between individuals and populations
  • The first principal component (PC1) typically separates populations with the greatest genetic differentiation
  • Subsequent components (PC2, PC3, etc.) capture additional patterns of variation
  • Individuals from the same population tend to cluster together in PCA plots

The PCA visualization you'll create in this lab reveals how genetic variation is structured across global populations and provides a framework for understanding an individual's genetic ancestry.

Fixation Index (FST)

FST is a measure of genetic differentiation between populations:

  • Values range from 0 (no differentiation) to 1 (complete differentiation)
  • Higher FST values indicate greater genetic differences between populations
  • FST calculations help quantify the degree of genetic isolation between populations

By calculating FST between population pairs in the lab exercises, you're measuring the genetic "distance" between populations, which reflects their evolutionary history and the degree of migration between them.

Application to Genetic Genealogy

Population References for Genealogical Analysis

The population genetic patterns you explore in this lab form the foundation for genetic genealogy applications:

  • Ancestry Estimation: Population reference panels enable algorithms to estimate an individual's genetic ancestry proportions
  • IBD Detection Context: Population allele frequencies inform the likelihood that shared segments are identical by descent (IBD) versus identical by state (IBS)
  • Relationship Inference: Population-specific genetic patterns help calibrate models for inferring genealogical relationships

Understanding these population patterns is crucial for accurately interpreting genetic connections between individuals, especially those with ancestry from multiple populations.

Anthropological Significance

From an anthropological perspective, population genetic patterns reflect human history and cultural processes:

  • Human Origins: The greater genetic diversity in African populations supports the "Out of Africa" model of human evolution
  • Migration Patterns: Genetic similarity between populations often correlates with historical migration routes
  • Social Structures: Patterns of genetic variation can reflect historical social practices (e.g., marriage patterns, cultural isolation)
  • Admixture Events: Genetic evidence of population mixing corresponds to historical events like colonization and forced migration

The 1000 Genomes data you'll analyze captures these historical processes through genetic patterns, connecting biological inheritance to human cultural and social history.

Beyond the Code

As you work through the coding exercises in this lab, keep in mind these deeper concepts:

  • Biological vs. Social Identity: Genetic patterns reflect biological inheritance but don't necessarily align with social or cultural identities
  • Statistical Nature: Population genetic patterns are statistical generalizations and individual genetic profiles may not perfectly match population averages
  • Temporal Dynamics: Genetic patterns change over time due to migration, admixture, and other processes
  • Interpretive Context: Genetic data must be interpreted within historical, social, and cultural contexts

These conceptual frameworks will help you move beyond simple data manipulation to a deeper understanding of what genetic patterns reveal about human relationships and history.
