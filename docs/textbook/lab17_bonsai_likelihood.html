Lab 17: Advanced Likelihood Calculations in Bonsai

Why This Matters: Likelihood calculations in Bonsai represent the convergence of mathematics, genetics, and cultural knowledge. They are the foundational instruments through which we translate genetic patterns into meaningful narratives of human connection. These calculations mediate between biological realities and social relationships, determining how genetic evidence is weighed when reconstructing complex networks of kinship, particularly in communities seeking to reconnect disrupted lineages.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand how statistical frameworks for relationship inference embody particular theories of relatedness
  • Analyze how different cultures conceptualize closeness of relation and how likelihood models reflect or challenge these understandings
  • Explore the ethical dimensions of uncertainty quantification in relationship reconstruction
  • Consider how Bayesian approaches to relationship inference incorporate culturally-specific prior knowledge
  • Examine how likelihood calculations negotiate the boundary between biological relationship and socially-constructed kinship
  • Reflect on the implications of statistical confidence when relationships have profound personal and community significance

Theoretical Foundations: Probability as Cultural Translation

At the heart of Bonsai's approach to relationship inference lies a profound act of cultural translation: the encoding of human relatedness as statistical likelihood. This translation is not neutral but emerges from specific epistemological traditions that prioritize quantification of uncertainty.

The Cultural Dimensions of Likelihood

The likelihood principle—that all evidence about a relationship is contained in the likelihood function—reflects a particular way of knowing that privileges mathematical formalism. This approach has both power and limitations:

L(R | D) = P(D | R)

where:

  • L(R | D) represents our belief in relationship R given the observed genetic sharing D
  • P(D | R) represents the probability of observing this pattern of genetic sharing if relationship R is true
  • D represents the genetic inheritance patterns observed between individuals
  • R represents a specific relationship category derived from Western kinship systems

This equation embodies a transformation—from the biological reality of shared DNA segments to culturally-recognized categories of relation. It is both a technical tool and a cultural artifact, reflecting particular understandings of how kinship should be categorized and measured.

Logarithmic Transformation as Epistemological Practice

Bonsai's use of logarithmic transformation in likelihood calculation is not merely a technical convenience but reflects deeper epistemological commitments about how we should represent certainty and uncertainty:

ln(L(R | D)) = ln(P(D | R))

This transformation offers several significant advantages that shape how we conceptualize relationships:

  • It creates a numerical framework where extremely rare relationships can still be represented
  • It transforms multiplicative uncertainty into additive uncertainty, changing how we combine evidence
  • It creates a scale where the difference between likelihood values becomes meaningful for comparison

The logarithmic scale creates a particular way of seeing relationships—one that compresses the distinction between extremely close relationships while expanding the ability to distinguish distant ones. This mathematical choice has profound implications for how we understand family structures and prioritize which relationships matter.

Relationship Models as Cultural Frameworks

The likelihood models embedded in Bonsai are not universal or neutral representations of human relationships. Rather, they encode specific cultural understandings of kinship that may align with or diverge from the diverse ways humans conceptualize family connections worldwide.

Parent-Child Relationship: The Binary Foundation

The parent-child likelihood model reflects assumptions about the genetic determinism of parenthood and its primacy in kinship structures. This model privileges a binary, biologically-determined relationship above all others:

The parent-child relationship model in Bonsai searches for a distinctive genetic signature: near-complete IBD1 sharing across the genome with minimal IBD2. This pattern reflects assumptions about biological parenthood that may not capture the complexity of parenting across cultures. In many societies, parenthood is a distributed responsibility, with multiple individuals fulfilling parental roles regardless of genetic connection. The statistical certainty with which Bonsai identifies parent-child relationships reinforces a particularly Western prioritization of biological over social parenthood.

When a genetic genealogist applies this model to communities with different kinship structures—such as those practicing communal childrearing or formal adoption customs—the algorithm may produce technically accurate but culturally misaligned results. These misalignments reveal how genetic kinship algorithms are not culturally neutral but participate in specific traditions of family recognition.

Sibling Relationships: Shared Origins as Social Bond

The sibling relationship model captures distinct patterns of IBD0, IBD1, and IBD2 sharing that reflect Western cultural emphasis on the full sibling bond as a core family relationship.

Siblinghood occupies a privileged place in many kinship systems, but how it is defined and valorized varies greatly. Bonsai's sibling likelihood model accommodates biological variation within its statistical framework but assumes a universal definition of what constitutes siblinghood. The classification of half-siblings as categorically different from full siblings reflects particular cultural assumptions about family formation that may conflict with indigenous kinship systems where such distinctions are understood differently or not recognized at all.

The statistical framework used to identify siblings—the Dirichlet-Multinomial distribution—offers flexibility but still operates within bounded categories of relationship. This probability model, while sophisticated, reinforces the idea that relationships must be discretely categorized rather than understood as positions within a continuous social network with culturally-specific meaning.

Distant Relationship Models: The Politics of Distance

The models Bonsai employs for detecting distant relationships reveal cultural assumptions about which connections matter and how connection attenuates across generations:

As we move to more distant relationships in Bonsai's framework, the models grow more complex, reflecting increasing statistical uncertainty. But this increasing uncertainty also mirrors cultural assumptions about the diminishing significance of relationships beyond immediate family. By modeling distant relationships with greater variance tolerances, the algorithm encodes a particular view of kinship as primarily nuclear and generationally bounded.

The calculation of "relationship coefficients" for cousins and other distant relations attempts to quantify connection in a way that may be at odds with how many communities understand extended kinship. For instance, in many Native American and African kinship systems, what Western models might call "second cousins" could hold relationship obligations equivalent to siblings. The variance allowed in the model increases with distance as well, suggesting that the precision with which we define relationships should diminish as genetic sharing decreases—a proposition that many cultural systems of kinship would contest.

Advanced Models: Negotiating Complexity and Diversity

The more sophisticated likelihood calculation techniques in Bonsai attempt to navigate the complexities of human diversity but bring their own cultural assumptions.

Multivariate Probability: The Multidimensionality of Kinship

Multivariate probability distributions in Bonsai represent an attempt to capture the multidimensional nature of kinship through a more complex mathematical lens:

Bonsai's multivariate approach recognizes that relationships cannot be reduced to a single dimension of genetic sharing but are characterized by complex patterns. This mathematical sophistication mirrors the social complexity of kinship, where relationships are defined through multiple dimensions of obligation, history, and connection. By incorporating multiple metrics—total sharing, segment counts, length distributions—these models create a more textured representation of relatedness that begins to approach the nuance of human understanding.

Yet, these metrics still prioritize certain dimensions of relationship while rendering others invisible. The selection of which genetic features matter in the algorithm's understanding of kinship creates a technological framework that privileges some aspects of relatedness over others. This selection process is not neutral but reflects research priorities and cultural assumptions about what constitutes meaningful connection between people.

Bayesian Integration: Prior Knowledge as Cultural Context

Bayesian approaches to relationship inference in Bonsai provide a formal framework for incorporating prior knowledge and cultural context:

The Bayesian paradigm offers a powerful way to formalize how cultural knowledge shapes our interpretation of genetic evidence. By encoding prior expectations about relationship frequencies or known family structures into the statistical framework, Bayesian methods provide a mathematical approach to integrating cultural context with biological data. This integration represents a significant step toward respecting the social dimensions of kinship within a computational framework.

However, the cultural assumptions embedded in prior distributions are rarely made explicit. Whose prior knowledge counts, and how it is quantified, remains a critical question. When working with historically marginalized communities, researchers must consider whether the prior distributions used in their models reflect colonial assumptions or community understandings of kinship. The mathematical formalism of Bayesian statistics can obscure these political dimensions, presenting culturally-specific assumptions as neutral parameters.

Global Structures: From Individual to Community

Bonsai's transition from pairwise relationships to global pedigree structures represents a shift from individual to community-level analysis that has profound implications for how we understand belonging.

Pedigree Likelihood: The Social Network as Statistical Entity

The global likelihood calculation for entire pedigrees attempts to mathematically represent the complex social networks that constitute communities:

When Bonsai calculates the likelihood of an entire pedigree structure, it moves beyond individual relationships to evaluate the plausibility of a complete network of connections. This holistic approach aligns with how many communities understand themselves—not as collections of discrete dyadic relationships but as interconnected networks of belonging. The mathematical formulation of pedigree likelihood represents an attempt to capture this social complexity within a statistical framework.

Yet the additivity assumption in pedigree likelihood calculation—where the global score is simply the sum of pairwise scores—may miss important aspects of how communities function. Real human communities exhibit emergent properties that cannot be reduced to the sum of individual relationships. Cultural practices around exogamy, endogamy, and preferred marriage patterns create structure at the community level that pair-focused algorithms may struggle to capture adequately.

Consistency Constraints: The Logic of Belonging

Relationship consistency constraints in pedigree reconstruction enforce a particular logical structure on kinship that may not align with all cultural understandings:

The consistency requirements in Bonsai's pedigree reconstruction reflect a specific logic of kinship—one where relationships must form coherent, non-contradictory networks. While this logical structure is necessary for computational tractability, it may impose a rigidity that fails to capture the fluid, contextual nature of kinship in many societies. For example, in some cultures, an individual might simultaneously occupy different kinship positions depending on context, ceremony, or social role—a flexibility that defies the strict logical hierarchies of computational pedigrees.

The algorithm's search for the "closest common ancestor" between individuals reflects particular assumptions about how kinship should be traced and measured. This approach prioritizes genetic distance over other forms of connection that might be more culturally salient, such as clan membership or ceremonial relationships that create non-genetic but deeply meaningful bonds of kinship.

Specialized Models: Engaging with Human Diversity

Bonsai's specialized likelihood models for endogamous populations and admixture represent attempts to account for human diversity within a statistical framework, revealing both the promise and limitations of computational approaches to complex cultural realities.

Endogamy: Statistical Adaptation to Cultural Practice

The endogamous population adjustments in Bonsai attempt to mathematically account for marriage patterns that shape genetic inheritance within particular communities:

Endogamy—the practice of marrying within a specific social group—creates distinctive patterns of genetic sharing that standard relationship models may misinterpret. Bonsai's endogamy adjustments represent an attempt to accommodate this cultural diversity within its statistical framework. By modifying expectations about background IBD sharing, these adjustments recognize that relationships exist within specific historical and cultural contexts that shape patterns of inheritance.

However, treating endogamy merely as a statistical "confounding factor" risks reducing rich cultural traditions to technical problems requiring correction. Many endogamous communities practice strategic marriage patterns tied to cultural continuity, resource management, or spiritual beliefs. By focusing solely on the genetic consequences without engaging the cultural meanings, computational approaches like Bonsai may inadvertently reinforce the marginalization of non-Western kinship systems as deviations from a statistical norm.

Admixture: The Genetics of Cultural Contact

Admixture-aware likelihood calculations in Bonsai reflect attempts to account for the genetic consequences of cultural contact, migration, and colonization:

Admixture-aware models represent Bonsai's effort to address the complexities of human history—migration, cultural contact, and the genetic consequences of colonization and forced displacement. By adjusting relationship likelihoods based on ancestry similarity, these models acknowledge that relationship inference operates differently across diverse populations with complex histories.

Yet the very framework of "admixture" itself emerges from particular historical conceptions of human groups as discrete, genetically distinct populations that then "mix"—a conceptualization with deep roots in colonial thinking about race and human difference. While the statistical adjustments may improve technical accuracy, their framing risks reinforcing problematic understandings of human diversity. More critically, these adjustments often focus on technical challenges of reference panel bias rather than engaging with how communities themselves understand and navigate complex ancestral backgrounds.

Interpreting Results: Navigating Uncertainty and Significance

How Bonsai guides users in interpreting likelihood results reveals particular approaches to uncertainty and confidence that have profound implications for individuals seeking to understand their genetic relationships.

Likelihood Ratios: The Quantification of Confidence

Likelihood ratio tests in Bonsai translate statistical confidence into language that shapes how users understand the certainty of relationship claims:

The translation of likelihood ratios into categorical statements of evidential strength—"very strong," "moderate," or "weak" evidence—represents a critical interface between mathematical uncertainty and human understanding. These verbal interpretations shape how users conceptualize their relationships, potentially transforming statistical nuance into perceived certainty. The thresholds chosen for these categories (100, 10, 3) reflect particular judgments about what constitutes meaningful confidence in relationship claims.

For individuals searching for family connections after historical trauma like slavery or forced adoption, these interpretations carry profound emotional weight. The difference between "strong evidence" and "moderate evidence" for a potential parent-child relationship could significantly impact someone's sense of identity and belonging. The responsibility of translating mathematical likelihoods into meaningful guidance requires both technical accuracy and deep ethical awareness of the human consequences of relationship inference.

Confidence Intervals: The Ethics of Uncertainty

Confidence intervals in Bonsai provide a framework for understanding uncertainty that carries ethical implications for how relationships are claimed or contested:

By calculating confidence intervals for relationship metrics, Bonsai acknowledges the inherent uncertainty in genetic relationship inference. This statistical humility represents an important ethical position—that biological evidence alone cannot provide absolute certainty about human relationships. The width of these confidence intervals increases with relationship distance, mirroring how certainty diminishes as we move beyond immediate family.

This approach to uncertainty has implications for how genetic evidence is used in sensitive contexts like tribal enrollment, inheritance claims, or family reunification. By making uncertainty explicit through confidence intervals, Bonsai invites consideration of how much certainty should be required for different relationship claims in different contexts. This statistical approach raises profound questions about the burden of proof for kinship and how different standards of evidence might apply to different kinds of relationship claims.

Beyond Algorithm: Customizing for Cultural Context

The ability to implement custom likelihood functions in Bonsai opens possibilities for adapting relationship inference to specific cultural contexts and priorities.

Cultural Adaptation of Algorithms

The template for custom likelihood functions represents an opportunity to move beyond universal models toward culturally-specific approaches to relationship inference:

Bonsai's framework for custom likelihood functions offers a pathway toward more culturally responsive genetic genealogy. This customization potential acknowledges that universal models of relationship cannot adequately capture the diversity of human kinship systems. By allowing researchers and communities to implement relationship models that reflect specific cultural understandings of kinship, this approach begins to address the epistemological limitations of standard algorithms.

For example, a community that recognizes clan relationships as equally significant to biological parenthood might implement custom functions that weight these connections differently. Indigenous communities with specific traditions around adoption might develop models that formalize their understanding of kinship beyond genetic coefficients. These customizations represent not just technical adjustments but potential acts of algorithmic sovereignty—allowing communities to encode their own understanding of relatedness into computational tools.

Integrating Non-Genetic Knowledge

The age-aware likelihood function example demonstrates how non-genetic information can be incorporated to create more contextually appropriate relationship inferences:

By incorporating age information into relationship likelihood calculation, Bonsai demonstrates how algorithms can integrate multiple forms of knowledge beyond genetics. This integration acknowledges that meaningful relationship inference requires contextual information about individuals and communities. The age-aware function shown penalizes biologically plausible but socially implausible relationships, bringing social reality into conversation with genetic evidence.

This approach could be extended to incorporate other forms of non-genetic knowledge that shape how relationships are understood within particular contexts—geographic proximity, historical records, oral histories, or community recognition. Such integration represents a more holistic approach to relationship inference that respects the multidimensional nature of human kinship systems and acknowledges that DNA alone provides an incomplete picture of how people understand their relationships to one another.

Broader Implications: Likelihood as Cultural Practice

Beyond its technical details, Bonsai's likelihood calculation framework embodies a particular approach to knowledge production with significant implications for how we understand human relationships.

The Politics of Relationship Categories

The relationship categories encoded in Bonsai's likelihood models reflect particular cultural understandings of kinship that may reproduce or challenge existing power structures:

By operationalizing specific relationship categories like "parent-child," "full siblings," and "first cousins," Bonsai's likelihood framework privileges particular ways of organizing and categorizing human connections. These categories emerge from Western kinship systems and may not align with how all communities conceptualize relationships. The mathematical formalization of these categories in likelihood calculations gives them a technical authority that can overshadow alternative kinship frameworks.

This categorical approach has particular significance for communities whose kinship systems were historically targeted by colonial powers. Many indigenous peoples experienced deliberate attempts to dismantle their kinship systems through policies of forced assimilation, boarding schools, and adoption. When genetic genealogy algorithms reinforce Western kinship categories without acknowledging alternatives, they risk participating in this ongoing epistemological colonization, presenting one cultural system of relationships as universal and scientifically validated.

Algorithmic Authority and Community Knowledge

The authority granted to likelihood-based relationship inferences raises questions about whose knowledge counts when determining family connections:

The mathematical sophistication of Bonsai's likelihood calculations can create an impression of objective, scientific authority that may overshadow community-based knowledge about relationships. In contexts where algorithmic inferences conflict with family narratives or community recognition, questions arise about which form of knowledge should take precedence. The statistical confidence expressed through likelihood ratios may be given disproportionate weight compared to oral histories or cultural practices of kinship recognition.

This tension becomes particularly significant in communities reconstructing relationships after historical trauma. For African Americans tracing ancestry through and beyond slavery, or Native communities reconnecting across generations of forced separation, genetic evidence provides an important but inherently limited window into complex histories of relationship. Responsible use of likelihood-based inference requires recognizing the complementary value of community knowledge and the limitations of statistical approaches to human connection.

Reflection: The likelihood calculations at the heart of Bonsai represent a profound attempt to translate between different systems of knowledge—from the biological reality of shared DNA to the social reality of human relationships. This translation is never neutral or complete but always shaped by cultural assumptions about what relationships matter and how they should be categorized. As we develop more sophisticated approaches to genetic relationship inference, we must continually examine whose understanding of kinship is privileged in our algorithms and how these technical choices participate in broader conversations about identity, belonging, and the diverse ways humans create meaningful connections with one another.
